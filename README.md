# EXP-3-PROMPT-ENGINEERING-

## Aim: 
Evaluation of 2024 Prompting Tools Across Diverse AI Platforms: 
ChatGPT, Claude, Bard, Cohere Command, and Meta
Experiment:
Within a specific use case (e.g., summarizing text, answering technical questions), compare the performance, user experience, and response quality of prompting tools across these different AI platforms.

## Algorithm:
1.Choose a Use Case – Select one task (e.g., summarization, coding Q&A, creative writing).

2.Design a Prompt – Write one clear, consistent prompt for all platforms.

3.Run the Prompt – Input the same prompt into ChatGPT, Claude, Bard (Gemini), Cohere, and Meta.

4.Collect Outputs – Record responses without modification.

5.Evaluate Responses – Assess based on accuracy, clarity, depth, creativity, and user experience.

6.Tabulate Results – Create a comparison table across platforms.

7.Analyze & Conclude – Identify strengths/weaknesses and decide which tool performs best.

## Prompt

Write a short story set in a world where memories can be traded like currency. Focus on a character who sells their happiest memory to escape debt, and explore how this decision affects their relationships, identity, and sense of purpose. Use vivid sensory details, internal monologue, and a twist that redefines what “value” truly means.

## Output

## The Price of Remembering

The market for memories was always busiest at dusk. Lanterns flickered along the narrow alley, illuminating glass vials that shimmered with swirling light. Each one contained a moment — laughter, pain, a kiss in the rain — ready to be bought, sold, or stolen.

Elara clutched the slip of paper with her debt amount scrawled across it. The numbers burned into her mind heavier than hunger. Just one vial… just one, and I’ll be free.

Inside the trading hall, the air smelled of copper and salt. Merchants whispered, “First love, still fresh… childhood summers, sun-warmed skin… a father’s lullaby, sung only once.” The memories pulsed faintly, as if alive.

Elara approached the broker’s desk. “I want to sell,” she said, her voice shaking.

“What memory?” His eyes were cold, metallic.

She hesitated. Behind her eyelids, she replayed it: the golden afternoon when she was twelve, chasing her younger brother through a meadow, laughter echoing as the world felt endless and safe. It was her anchor in every storm.

“My happiest one.”

The broker didn’t flinch. He drew the memory out through a thin glass needle, and she felt it drain — warmth leaving her chest, color dulling at the edges of the world. The vial glowed so brightly she had to look away.

When the transaction ended, her debt was gone. She stumbled into the street, pockets lighter but free at last. Or so she thought.

At home, her brother greeted her with a grin. “Remember that day in the meadow? When we—”

Elara blinked. A hole yawned inside her. The image was gone, replaced by blankness. She forced a smile. “I… don’t recall.”

His face fell, confusion clouding his joy. “It was the happiest I’ve ever seen you.”

The weeks that followed were colder. Friends spoke of her kindness, her warmth, but she felt like an actor reciting lines she no longer believed. Without that core memory, her laughter sounded hollow, her touch mechanical. She was debt-free, yet untethered, a ghost wandering through her own life.

What am I, without what I remember? she wondered, staring at her reflection.

Months later, she returned to the market. The meadow memory sat on display, more expensive now than any jewel. She watched strangers bid for it, hungering for joy that was never theirs. She couldn’t afford to buy it back.

But as she stood there, she noticed something. A little girl tugged at her mother’s sleeve, pointing at the vial, her eyes alight with wonder. Elara realized: though the memory was gone from her, it could still create joy in another.

For the first time, she smiled — not out of habit, but from a strange, tender understanding. Value wasn’t in owning happiness, but in how it rippled outward, reshaping others.

Elara turned away from the auction, empty yet somehow full. She would build new memories, not to hoard, but to give.

Because in a world where memories were currency, she had discovered a richer truth:
the worth of a life isn’t what you keep — it’s what you leave behind.

## The Weight of Gold

The extraction chair hummed with the soft whir of expensive machinery, its chrome surface cold against Maya's bare arms. She closed her eyes and tried not to think about what she was about to lose.
"Just relax," Dr. Vance murmured, adjusting the neural interface crown on her head. The metal felt heavier than it should have, like a lead tiara. "Think of your happiest memory. Let it fill your mind completely."
Maya's breath caught as the scene bloomed behind her eyelids: her daughter Lily's sixth birthday, three years ago. The smell of vanilla cake baking in their tiny kitchen. Lily's delighted shriek as she unwrapped the secondhand bicycle they couldn't afford but bought anyway. The weight of her daughter's arms around her neck, sticky fingers tangling in her hair, whispering "Thank you, Mama" in that breathless voice children use when joy overwhelms them.
The memory was worth fifty thousand credits—enough to clear her debts and keep their apartment for another year. Enough to buy Lily new shoes, medicine when she got sick, maybe even a real birthday cake instead of the boxed mix.
"Beginning extraction," Dr. Vance announced.
Maya felt the memory lifting from her mind like smoke, its edges growing fuzzy and distant. The sensation of Lily's embrace faded first, then the sound of her laughter, until only facts remained: there had been a birthday, there had been a bicycle, there had been happiness. But the feeling—that golden, effervescent joy—was gone, packaged now in a crystalline memory chip worth more than Maya had ever owned.
Walking home through the city's neon-drenched streets, Maya touched her pocket where the credit transfer card sat. She should have felt relief, but instead there was only a strange hollowness, as if someone had scooped out part of her chest.
"Mom?" Lily looked up from her homework as Maya entered their cramped apartment. "You look different."
Maya studied her daughter's face—the same dark eyes, the same stubborn cowlick that never lay flat. She knew she loved this child, had sacrificed everything for her, but the knowledge felt academic now, a truth she understood but couldn't quite feel.
"I'm fine, sweetheart. Just tired."
But she wasn't fine. Over the following weeks, Maya found herself going through the motions of motherhood like an actor who'd forgotten her lines. She made Lily's lunches, helped with homework, tucked her into bed each night, but something essential was missing. The joy had been the thread that connected all her sacrifices to their meaning, and without it, parenthood felt like an endless series of obligations.
Lily noticed. Children always do.
"Why don't you smile anymore?" she asked one evening, her small hand tugging at Maya's sleeve. "You used to smile when you looked at me."
Maya's throat constricted. "I'm sorry, baby. Mama's just... figuring some things out."
"Did you sell a happy memory?" Lily's question was matter-of-fact, as if she were asking about the weather. "Tommy's dad sold one and now he doesn't laugh at Tommy's jokes anymore."
The credit card in Maya's pocket felt like it was burning through the fabric. "How do you know about that?"
"Everyone knows. Mrs. Chen says it's what poor people do when they run out of choices." Lily's voice was small but steady. "Was I your happiest memory?"
Maya knelt beside her daughter's chair, taking those small hands in hers. "Yes," she whispered. "You were."
"And you sold me?"
The words hit like physical blows. "No, Lily. I sold the memory, not you. You're still here. I still love you."
"But you don't remember loving me." It wasn't a question.
Maya opened her mouth to deny it, then closed it. Lily deserved honesty, even when it cut.
"I remember that I love you," Maya said carefully. "I know that you're the most important thing in my world. I know that I would do anything for you—I just did. But the feeling... that's harder now."
Lily nodded solemnly, too wise for her nine years. "Can you buy it back?"
"No, sweetheart. Memory trading doesn't work that way."
That night, Maya lay awake staring at the ceiling, counting the sound of Lily's breathing in the next room. Fifty thousand credits sat in her account, but she felt poorer than ever. She'd traded her capacity for joy for financial security, and now she wondered if she'd gotten the worse end of the deal.
The next morning brought an unexpected visitor. Dr. Vance stood at her door, looking uncomfortable.
"Ms. Rodriguez, I need to speak with you about your memory extraction."
Maya's blood chilled. "Is there a problem with the transfer? Because I already used some of the credits—"
"No, nothing like that." Dr. Vance stepped inside uninvited, glancing around their modest apartment. "I have a confession to make. I've been in this business for fifteen years, extracting memories from people who need the money. Happy memories, sad ones, traumatic experiences people want to forget. I've seen it all."
Maya waited, a knot forming in her stomach.
"Your memory," Dr. Vance continued, "was the most beautiful I've ever encountered. The joy in it was... pure. Selfless. When I experienced it during the quality check, it reminded me why I became a doctor in the first place. Why I wanted to help people."
"I don't understand."
Dr. Vance pulled out a small device Maya recognized—a personal memory player, the kind only the wealthy could afford. "I bought your memory. Not for resale, but for myself. I've been... struggling with depression, you see. Lost my way. But your memory of loving your daughter, of finding perfect happiness in her joy—it's been like medicine."
Maya stared at the device. Inside that small machine was her capacity for joy, playing on loop for a stranger.
"I came here to see the child who inspired such love," Dr. Vance continued. "And to make you an offer."
From her briefcase, she withdrew a memory chip—different from the one Maya's joy was stored in, this one glowing with a soft blue light.
"This contains my memory of receiving my medical degree. The day I realized I could spend my life helping people heal. It's not the same as what you lost, but it might help you find meaning in sacrifice again. And..." She paused, seeming to wrestle with herself. "I'd like to make regular payments for the right to keep your memory. A sort of... rental fee. It would be enough to support you and Lily comfortably."
Maya looked through the doorway where Lily sat at the kitchen table, carefully drawing a picture. Even without the golden glow of that perfect birthday memory, Maya could see the beauty in her daughter's concentration, the way her tongue poked out when she focused, the gentle curve of her small shoulders.
"Keep the memory," Maya said quietly. "If it's helping you help others, then maybe it's doing more good in your hands than it ever did in mine."
Dr. Vance's eyes widened. "But the rental fee—"
"I'll accept that. But I don't need your memory in return." Maya smiled, and was surprised to find it felt real. "I'm realizing that joy isn't just something you feel—it's something you choose to create. My happiest memory with Lily happened because I decided her happiness mattered more than our bank account. That choice is still mine to make, even without the memory of how it felt."
As if summoned by their conversation, Lily appeared in the doorway. "Mom? Who's this lady?"
"Someone who's helping Mama help you," Maya said, crouching to Lily's level. "How would you like to go get ice cream? With sprinkles?"
Lily's face lit up with the same pure joy Maya remembered from that long-ago birthday, and Maya felt an answering warmth bloom in her chest. Not the golden radiance of her traded memory, but something quieter and more sustainable: the satisfaction of choosing love over fear, again and again.
Dr. Vance watched them embrace and quietly slipped out, leaving the memory chip on the table. Maya never used it. She discovered she didn't need to borrow someone else's sense of purpose when she was busy creating her own, one small moment of chosen joy at a time.
Later, watching Lily savor her ice cream with the intense concentration only children possess, Maya realized she'd learned something the memory traders never advertised: the most valuable memories aren't the ones you've already made, but the ones you're brave enough to create next.

## The price of a sunset
The humming of the debt-collector’s notice was a physical thing, vibrating through the floorboards and rattling the empty food tins on the counter. Elara winced, pressing her hands against her ears, a futile attempt to block out the relentless, electronic drone. It was a sound that had been a part of her life for as long as she could remember, and the only way to silence it was with a full-balance deposit at a Memory Exchange. Her last remaining asset was a single, shimmering vial, no bigger than her thumb. It held the memory of a perfect sunset.

The clinic was all smooth marble and sterile light. The air smelled of antiseptic and forgotten dreams. A memory-broker with a face as blank as a new ledger took the vial from her. "High-grade," he murmured, holding it up to the light. Inside, the memory swirled, a helix of brilliant oranges, deep purples, and the faint, sweet scent of her grandmother's cinnamon cakes. It was the last afternoon they spent together, sitting on the porch swing, watching the sky bleed into a masterpiece. Her younger brother, Jax, was there too, laughing as he chased fireflies.

The broker's machine was a cold, silver thing with a single, glowing aperture. "Place your finger here," he instructed, his voice devoid of warmth. As she did, she felt a subtle, almost spiritual tug. It wasn't painful, but it was a profound emptiness, like a ghost had left her body. The image of the sunset faded from her mind's eye. The cinnamon scent was gone. Even the laughter of her brother was a hollow echo she could no longer hear. The machine blinked. A small, clear credit chip slid into a slot. The debt-collector's hum instantly ceased.

For the first few days, the silence was a relief. The weight was gone. She could buy food, pay her rent, and even afford a new pair of boots. But a new kind of silence settled inside her. Her brother, Jax, called and started a story with, "Remember that time Grams baked us a dozen cakes?" A wave of confusion washed over her. She could recall the fact of the cakes, the fact of the day, but the feeling, the joyous, visceral memory of it—that was gone. She could only stare blankly, her smile a brittle facade. A chasm opened between them, and he stopped calling.

She tried to revisit the porch, to recreate the moment, but the sunset was just a sunset, a pretty but lifeless collection of colors. The feeling of her grandmother's warm hand was just a concept now, not a sensation. A part of her identity, tied to that moment of perfect peace and love, had vanished. She was free of debt, but she was adrift, untethered.

One evening, staring out at a sky that felt utterly meaningless, she saw the memory-broker again, this time at a small, unassuming teahouse. He wasn't wearing his clinical uniform. He looked at her, and his blank expression softened.

"You sold the Sunset memory," he said quietly, a statement, not a question. "It was the highest value we've seen all year. Its worth wasn't in the vividness of the colors or the warmth of the day. Its worth was in the profound love that memory held. The system doesn't just measure a memory's clarity; it measures its emotional weight."

He leaned forward, his voice a low whisper. "We don't sell the most valuable ones. We trade them to a network of caregivers and healers, those who use the emotional residue of pure, selfless love to help the lost and broken. Your memory is now a comfort to a lonely elder, a balm for a child in distress. Its true value wasn't the currency it gave you, but the purpose it now serves in the world. You didn't just sell a memory; you gave away a piece of your love, and it saved someone else."

Elara looked at her hands, no longer seeing a void, but a profound and invisible echo. The chip she held was not just a payment for a debt, but a testament to the immense capacity for love she once had. And in that moment, she realized her new purpose wasn't to mourn what was gone, but to live a life that could create something so valuable again.


## The Price of Joy

The air in the Memory Exchange smelled of copper and desperation, a metallic tang that clung to the back of Elara’s throat. The room was dim, lit only by the soft glow of holographic memories floating above the counters—swirling auroras of gold for happiness, deep blues for sorrow, and muted grays for the mundane. Elara clutched her token tightly, a small crystal orb containing her happiest memory: the day her father had taught her to fly a kite on the windswept cliffs of their coastal village. The kite’s tail had danced like a ribbon of sunlight, and her father’s laughter had been warm and unburdened.

“Final offer,” the trader said, his voice flat. “Five years of debt cleared. But once it’s gone, it’s gone.”

Elara’s fingers trembled. *Five years. Freedom.* But at what cost? She closed her eyes, replaying the memory one last time. The salt on her lips, the rough weave of the kite string, her father’s calloused hand brushing hers. Tears prickled, but she blinked them away. Debt had already taken her home, her savings, her sleep. She couldn’t let it take her future too.

“Deal,” she whispered, handing over the orb.

The trader’s fingers closed around it, and the room seemed to dim further, as if a light had been snuffed out. Elara’s chest ached, hollow and cold. She left the Exchange, the weight of her decision pressing on her shoulders like a stone.

At first, the relief was sweet. The debt collectors stopped hounding her. She could afford a warm meal, a proper bed. But then the questions began. Her best friend, Mira, noticed the change first. “You’re… distant,” she said one evening, her brow furrowed. “Like you’re not all here.”

Elara forced a smile. “Just tired. Work’s been rough.”

But it wasn’t just Mira. Elara found herself struggling to connect with anyone. Conversations felt hollow, laughter forced. She’d always been the one to light up a room, to remind others of the beauty in small moments. Now, she felt like a ghost, drifting through her own life.

One night, she dreamed of the kite. But when she reached for the memory, it was gone—just an empty void where warmth should have been. She woke with a start, her heart pounding. *What have I done?*

The twist came weeks later, when she received a letter from her father. He was ill, the doctors said, and time was short. Elara rushed to his bedside, her mind racing. She hadn’t spoken to him in months, too ashamed of her failure to admit she’d sold the memory of him.

As she sat by his bed, he took her hand, his grip frail but steady. “I know what you did,” he said, his voice soft but clear. “I felt it the day it happened. A piece of me… went missing.”

Elara’s breath caught. “I’m sorry. I was drowning. I didn’t know what else to do.”

Her father smiled, a shadow of the man who’d once flown kites with her. “Memories aren’t just ours, Elara. They’re shared. And sometimes, their value isn’t in keeping them, but in passing them on.”

He reached under his pillow and pulled out a small, worn journal. “I’ve been writing down everything I could remember of that day. For you. So you’d never lose it entirely.”

Tears streamed down Elara’s face as she opened the journal. The pages were filled with sketches of the kite, descriptions of the wind, even a pressed flower from the cliffside. It wasn’t the same as the memory, but it was something more—a bridge between them, a reminder that love couldn’t be traded away.

In the weeks that followed, Elara began to rebuild. She stopped seeing memories as currency and started seeing them as threads that connected her to others. She shared stories of her father with Mira, with strangers, with anyone who would listen. And slowly, the hollow ache in her chest began to fill—not with the memory she’d sold, but with something new: purpose.

The world still traded memories, but Elara knew now that their true value wasn’t in their price, but in their ability to bind people together. She’d sold her happiest memory, but in doing so, she’d discovered something far more precious: the power of letting go, and the strength of holding on.

## Akira's story

The market buzzed with people haggling over memories like they were fresh produce. Scents of cardamom and cinnamon wafted through the air, mingling with whispers of forgotten moments. Amidst the chaos, Akira stood at her stall, eyes fixed on the small, golden vial in her hand. It contained her happiest memory – the day her daughter took her first steps.
Akira's debt collector, Mr. Kaul, had been breathing down her neck for months. She owed him a small fortune, and her business was on the verge of collapse. The vial, filled with the warmth of sunlight and her daughter's giggles, was her only valuable asset left. She unscrewed the cap, releasing the memory's essence. The smell of freshly cut grass and her daughter's baby shampoo filled her nostrils.
"What will you give me for this?" Akira asked the buyer, a young woman with a hunger in her eyes.
The woman's gaze lingered on the vial before responding, "I'll give you enough to clear your debt and a little more. But are you sure you're ready to let it go?"
Akira's mind replayed the memory – her daughter's wobbly steps, the feel of tiny hands grasping her fingers, the sound of her child's laughter. She recalled the pride and joy that swelled in her chest. But the weight of her debt was crushing her. She nodded, and the woman handed over a pouch of silver coins.
As Akira walked away from the market, she felt a void within her. She couldn't recall the last time she'd thought about her daughter's first steps without tearing up. Now, it was just a memory someone else owned. Her relationships began to fray – her daughter sensed her distant mood, and her friends noticed her withdrawn nature.
Akira wandered aimlessly, questioning her identity. Who was she without that memory? What defined her if not the moments that brought her joy? The city seemed to grow darker, the shadows deepening as she lost her sense of purpose.
Days turned into weeks, and Akira's life became a monotony of gray skies and hollow smiles. She'd visit her daughter, but the interactions felt forced, like she was acting out a role she'd forgotten. One evening, while walking through the market, Akira saw the young woman who'd bought her memory. She was smiling, eyes closed, as she relived the moment Akira had sold.
Akira approached her, curiosity getting the better of her. "Do you mind if I ask...does it still feel the same?"
The woman opened her eyes, and for a moment, Akira saw her daughter's laughter reflected there. "It's...different. The joy is still there, but it's tinged with the knowledge that it's not mine originally. Yet, it's given me hope."
Akira's gaze lingered on the woman's face, and she realized that her happiest memory had found a new home, bringing joy to someone else's life. The value wasn't in owning the memory but in the ripple effect it created.
As she turned to leave, Akira noticed a small, forgotten stall tucked away in a corner. An old man sat there, eyes twinkling, as he offered memories for free. "Take one," he said, offering a vial filled with the scent of rain and the sound of children's laughter. "Sometimes, the most valuable things in life are the ones we give away."
Akira took the vial, and as she uncapped it, she felt a sense of peace wash over her. She realized that her true wealth wasn't in the memories she owned but in the connections she made and the joy she could bring to others. The old man's words echoed in her mind – value wasn't about possession; it was about the impact.
From that day forward, Akira's life began to transform. She started a new business, sharing memories that brought people together. Her relationships healed, and her sense of purpose was rekindled. She learned that sometimes, letting go of what's most precious can lead to finding something far more valuable – the beauty of human connection and the memories yet to be made.
